The coffee shop was busy as usual. I waved at the hostess, Jan, as we walked to the counter. She was busy folding napkins at her stand and talking to her coworkers. Most of the tables and lounge areas were already taken. The seated counter had a few regulars talking or interacting with their augmented reality (AR) units. Art pieces of all types were hanging on the walls. Large sculptures were standing on the floor and smaller ones on the coffee tables.

The shop was part of the local co-op group and provided a space for its artists. With universal basic income (UBI) no one really had to work but being part of any co-op and contributing let you earn credits to spend in member co-ops for anything produced, like hand made clothes or coffee. They called the co-op only currency coopers. Government officials didn’t like the alternative currency at first but when the extra tax revenue started to make up a significant portion of their budgets the opposition quickly quieted.

Hal was at his usual spot behind the big long counter that took up one whole side of the coffee shop. He was a tall man, with a big toothy grin, thick horn rimmed AR glasses and always wearing a baseball cap to cover his bald head.

Most restaurants and bars were all fully automated and many people visited them because it was less expensive than the co-operatives. But if you had coopers it felt good to spend them back in a way that helped your friends, the community and the social interaction with all the locals was always rewarding.

Hal looked up from his espresso machine, “Heeeyyy Paul! Good to see ya. The usual today? What about your friend?”

Hal was always so friendly it made me smile and I returned his hospitality with a grin, “Hey Hal! This is my brother-in-law David. His wife Susan and Karen are outside. We’re going to have our usuals. Thanks!”

Hal quickly wiped his hands and reached his long arm over the counter to shake David’s hand. “Nice to meet ya, what’ll it be? Hold on now, let me guess …” as he put his hands on his hips and quickly scanned David up and down. “Hmm, an Americano with sugar?”

Before David could respond Hal looked outside and raised his eyebrows, “Oh, that your wife waving her arms about and talking with Karen?

David and I turned around to look out the large picture window. Susan was talking with Karen in her usual animated way and literally had her arms above her head and was moving them back and forth talking about who knows what.

David smiled and chuckled, “Oh yeah, that’s her.”

“I’m going say Mocha latte with some whipped cream and cinnamon sprinkles …extra hot?” said Hal with an inquisitive look on his face.

David’s mouth was agape. “How the heck? Um, yes, I think she’ll like that. Thanks,” said David with a smile.

I laughed, “Coffee is this man’s life David, he’s had a lot of practice guessing what people like.”

“That and I think he’s beta testing some artificial intelligence (AI) customer prediction algorithms,” I added quietly.

Hal heard me and chided, “Damn it Paul, you’re giving away all my secrets!”

“Well …you’re lucky you’re such a regular.”

Hal shook his head slightly, smiled and waved us on, “Now get going, I’ve gotten a little behind baby sitting you two,” as he slid back behind his equipment.

David and I moved down to the large display case where there was a wide array of pastries, cakes, pies, muffins and scones. We placed our orders and waited off to the side.

“So how’s work been?” I asked.

“Oh, I’ve enjoyed being part of the training programs, but the work itself; it’s just not the same anymore,” David said as he slowly shook his head.

“The new exoskeletons we use to handle the high voltage stuff make the job too damn easy.”

He started to get agitated as he continued, “Safety mechanisms over safety mechanisms and the friggin’ AR displays spoon-feed you every step—I mean, you don’t have to think for yourself anymore. Sure,” David shrugged, “it helps with the insurance costs and there is hardly ever an incident now.”

“But, man, you just don’t get the same feel as when you’re working with your own hands,” his open hands flexed to fists and back open again.

“And the dang kids, they’re not learning to respect the dangers.”

“I mean it’s not like the old days when you weren’t careful and got zzzapped by a 440 volt line,” said David through pursed lips as he locked up his body and face.

He slowly relaxed and laughed, “Yeah, you were sore as hell after that and felt it for days. Made you real careful you know.”

I nodded and agreed, “Mmm-hmm, I know what you mean about it not being the same, I mean there’s hardly anything left for people to do in Information Technology (IT) anymore.”

“We used to design, architect and plan out what we’d be building or upgrading and how it would help out the organization. If was fun, engaging and creative work most of the time.”

“The AIs can design all the infrastructure, build the software faster, document everything and all without any errors. But on the bright side,” I smiled, “there’s no layer of incompetent management anymore either. We just make sure that electricity you work on is flowing and the infrastructure the AIs need keeps running.”

Everyone always said it was the self-driving cars that had started the AI takeover. It made sense to think that. They caused the first big waves of unemployment. With their extremely low accident rates, the autonomous vehicles drastically reduced the insurance costs of companies that ran large fleets. Except for some mechanics, the labor costs where practically zero. So every fleet, large or small, had to have them just to stay competitive.

The companies that ran fleets of vehicles helped themselves by perfecting the technology. Then it spread to the masses. People loved the idea of having their own limousine service. No more stress dealing with the traffic and idiots on the road. You could spend your drive time in more meaningful ways; doing work, social media, entertainment, talking, reading, playing games, or just taking a nap.

Then it rapidly spread to other jobs outside of transportation. Any job that was done behind a screen or a phone was quickly taken over. Every AI that was proven to perform better than a human in the same task was immediately adopted, otherwise you would no longer be competitive. The AIs could create and optimize new AIs with exponential speed and efficiency. It seemed like the takeover was done before it even started.

What people didn’t realize was that the real cause of the AI takeover wasn’t all the added efficiency and cost savings, it was really the lawyers. If you choose a human to perform the same task that an AI could do better, you would be found negligible at best and liable at worst. With that, the lawyers found a whole new revenue stream in all the lawsuits. Businesses had no other choice but to choose an AI over a human. Because when an AI was created to do a job, it was always better than a human in that same position. Open and shut case.

Once the lawsuits started it snowballed from there and it wasn’t long before it was understood that the safe choice, really, the only choice, was to get an AI for the job. Seemingly overnight the common sense became the fact that humans could never do the work with the same efficiency, the same quality, or with the same low cost as an AI, so you would be stupid to ever hire and have to deal with pesky humans. The AIs could work 24/7. No office, no insurance, no complaints, no demands and virtually no costs except for some IT infrastructure, connectivity and electricity.

The funniest part was how every smug manager that had been so quick to adopt the new AIs was laid off along with everyone else. They saw the massive reduction in labor costs, the huge increases in productivity and drooled—imagining huge bonuses would be their reward. Their only compensation was a notice that their employment would be terminated as well. In their short-sightedness the managers and administrators didn’t realize they were making themselves obsolete in the process.

Who needs people to manage AIs, when another AI can manage the workload better? Then with so many people out of work, no one had money to buy what was being so efficiently produced and so UBI was quickly made into law, put forward by AI lawyers and backed by AI economists of course.

Those wrinkles, that botox and other “cures” promised to wipe away, told me that this person had spent her life smiling and laughing in the sun. How could that be bad? It sounded like a good life to me.

I must’ve been spacing off again when Karen rubbed my knee, “So Paul, what is this show about again?”

“Oh, it’s not a big production. Most of the pieces were done by the local artist co-op. There’s a lot of old technology like tablets, desktop computers, clunky monitors and keyboards. Basically re-purposed junk.” I replied with a smile.

“The cool part though is how they added in some AR and other bits of new technology.”

I had helped out the artists over the last few weeks as they were putting their pieces together and working out the bugs with the old technology. All the artists were a lot younger and needed someone that knew the sedimentary layers of IT to help with the old wired networks, interfaces and cabling.

“I even had to contact a few old coworkers to find the connectors we needed to hook it all up. Besides the techy stuff there are a few other pieces mixed in as well. Think you guys will like it.”

The gallery was only a couple of blocks away. We walked slowly, enjoying our coffee and the beautiful fall colors of the trees. The old world of black asphalt and hard concrete was just a bleak memory to me. Years ago, all pedestrian areas had their hardscapes removed and replaced with cobblestones or pavers.

Another AI suggestion that allowed water and air to get to the soil and for large trees, food forests, flower, herb and vegetable gardens to be grown right next to buildings, homes and restaurants. It was fascinating how rapidly the landscape had transformed from a continuous dirty parking lot to a huge meandering nature park.

Pedestrian zones were also immensely popular as they provided a much needed refuge from the constant hum, whir and beeps of drones in the air and robotic vehicles on the ground. They were never far away but in these spaces they were kept out of sight and in designated routes behind alleys or underground.

Our flat was in the residential zone that surrounded the city center in a sprawling haphazard circle of multilevel tenancies. A central transportation hub connected our city with the other towns in our region and linked up with other hubs in larger cities. All had similar layouts to ours.

The local neighborhood of co-ops that Hal’s coffee shop was part of was an AI creation as well. The local news covered it extensively—a design for a complete system of human owned and operated businesses that, when interconnected and healthy, would self-sustain themselves. UBI was fine for the necessities, but people were discontented and felt lost. Many people had little meaning in their lives as it seemed like there was no point in doing anything since the AIs would always do it better. Humans just had no way to compete with the machines. So many people occupied their time by entertaining themselves with virtual reality (VR), AR, food, video games, drugs, or pornography.

Naturally an AI was given the task of solving the public health issue of having a large percentage of the population being out of work and clinically depressed. Its solution was to create the co-operative network to tap into the growing resentment against AIs. The co-ops helped people reconnect with each other, their local communities, and to gain some purpose and meaning in their lives.

The system was optimized so that the production of one co-op would feed into the public space or be consumed by another co-op. It created a self renewing and interconnected system of store fronts and businesses. The AI design was extremely thorough of course. All the policies and procedures were laid out for the well-being of the worker-owners and in turn the entire co-op.

It was geared to bring out the best possible work-life balance. Taking into account people’s personalities and their goals for themselves and their families. Based on the latest science and built on self-determination theory, it had proved more successful than anyone ever imagined. Attrition rates were the lowest ever recorded.

No one was getting rich of course. No one was asked to work more than 6 hours a week or one full shift. Most owners averaged 16 hours a week, so they could earn more coopers. Everyone involved reported extremely high levels of fulfillment and greatly enjoyed working with their friends and families. Some people just dove into their work and loved it so much they worked more than they would in an old full time job but it was always their choice to do so. It was good to see the AI technology finally being used to benefit common people and helping to enrich their lives for a change.

We entered the gallery space with its off-white walls and soft warm lighting. There were just a few people milling about silently observing the art. The gallery wasn’t large, only about three big rooms. The displays were all interesting in the way the artist’s had brought the old technology that was so fixed in screens and keyboards and melded it with the interactive virtual and augmented realities of today’s world. We started our leisurely walk around, contemplating the works.

David and Susan were standing in front of a piece chatting with, thankfully, only muted bursts of Susan’s laughter. Karen had to take a phone call, probably from the hospital. So I looked around and saw there was a little antechamber that was off to the side of the hallway near the bathrooms. I had noticed that the room’s door was locked when I was helping setup in the previous week, but had thought it was just for some storage.

As I entered, it seemed like the lights were brighter than the rest of the gallery. In the middle of the room was just one piece. A small door and its frame that were painted glossy white. It was about three feet high, two feet wide and only a few inches deep. It was sitting on a large white box to elevate it up to eye level.

“What’s this!” Karen exclaimed as she startled me and I jumped a little.

“Ha! Got you Paul,” she beamed and grabbed me a little around the waist as she moved her face over my shoulder to look at the piece.

“Not sure what it is. It kind of looks like the door from that old black and white television show. The one about the phantom zone or something.”

I shrugged and said, “It wasn’t here when I helped setup and, honestly, it doesn’t look like much.”

Karen had her brow furrowed and suddenly relaxed, “Oh, this is a traveling piece.”

She was reading from her AR unit. You could tell it was active by the small bright light on the front. Hers was one of the smaller units that sat on your ear like the old phone headsets. The AR projector was a thin wire that came out from the earpiece to the front of Karen’s eye. It shone directly onto a person’s retina and provided the visual overlay. Many people had larger AR devices that were incorporated into their eye glasses like mine was.

Karen continued reading, “The AR docent says it’s some new space age material created in a laboratory.”

Karen started to move her hands back and forth and made googly eyes, “Eww, Paul, I’m from space what mysteries are behind my door?” she finished by humming the old television show’s jingle.

“Hah, quit it! It’s just a door …dork!” I said but couldn’t help but smile and shake my head at her performance.

Hanging on the door knob was a small sign that read, “Pull here”

I glanced at Karen questionably. “Should we?”

“Mmm-hmm, AR says it’s okay.”

The handle wasn’t latched, so I gently pulled the miniature knob and slowly opened the door. There was just a wall of weird blackness behind it taking up the entire frame. We stepped a few feet back to get a better view.

“Oh—that is dark!” said Karen in a low tone as she crossed her arms and moved her hand over her mouth.

It was really strange to look at and in an unsettling way, unnerving. We stood there in silence for a minute taking it in—it was just so black. It was completely two-dimensional and it felt out of place in our three-dimensional world. I moved to the side to look at it at an angle, but the total flat perspective didn’t change. Even though the back wall was several feet away, it looked like the black continued from the door frame all the way to the wall. I wanted to explore and dive into it to see what else there was behind it. But the absolute blackness had a strange solidity.

I slowly walked around the platform to see if there was anything else to it. The back of the door was just painted white, nothing remarkable. Except I noticed there was some small type in the middle of the back of the door that I had to move in to read:

Cut doors and windows for a house; It is the empty spaces which make it useful.

Lao Tzu, Tao Te Ching

“It’s called Mondoblack©.”

I straightened up to look at Karen.

“It’s some new expensive nanotube substance, guess that’s why it’s not a full size door. It says it absorbs almost 100% of all the light hitting it, making it the blackest substance ever created. It looks so weird—so black. Honestly, I don’t really like it. I mean, it’s just too flat and empty.”

Karen then crossed her arms and tilted her head slightly to one side and said, “Umm, maybe if they did something a little more creative with it like add some color. Eww, or how about a creepy hand coming out of the dark?”

It was then that David and Susan entered the room. They both had furrowed brows. Susan spoke haltingly, “This thing looks odd—I sort of like the contrast with the white room because, um geez, it’s just so black.”

Susan’s brow relaxed and her face suddenly became animated, “Oh! Can I get shoes in that color?”

We all laughed a little nervously. David’s brow was still slightly furrowed.

“Well, I don’t really get it. It’s kind of simplistic. Like not much effort was put into it. I mean, that’s all there is, a door with just flat black in the frame?”

David walked around to the back of the door and read the quote, “Huh, that’s interesting.”

“A while back I helped assist an electrician’s apprentice class with a group going in for their next round of training and certifications. We had this quirky physics professor that came in for a short lecture.”

“Quirky huh? Was he as nerdy as Paul?” said Susan as she smiled at me to see how I reacted.

“Well,” David continued, “he was a little awkward, but um …let’s just say his hygiene was a bit lacking.”

“Anyways, this professor talked about the underlying science of electricity, electrons and why the equations were the way they were, yadda yadda.”

“He went on to say that everything there is, everything that we see and touch, really all matter, even something as dense and heavy as lead, was 99.999% empty space …”

David looked at the black doorway with a questionable expression on his face and in a pensive tone said, “Maybe all that emptiness in the space among the atoms is as black as that.”

While David was talking, I was staring at the doorway, trying to see if I could find a blemish or any reflection of light, but there was nothing there. Usually I keep my AR unit off because it just distracts me, but I had turned it on to see what it would say. It just spat out the same information about Mondoblack© as Karen had read. As I tried to have it zoom into the blackness, it gave a focus error. I turned the helpless thing off.

I felt an urge to just look at the Mondoblack© without trying to make sense of it anymore. As David spoke, I gazed into the doorway. A feeling of dizziness was growing. Was the doorway getting bigger? It seemed like I was moving toward it. Like after you run on a treadmill or get off a fast moving tram and there is that moment of disorientation before your brain catches up to the fact that you’re not moving anymore. I shook my head a little and blinked fast a few times. Probably just needed some water I thought and then Susan interrupted in her own special way.

She gave David a little jab at his comment, “Okay now Davey Downer, this thing is creeping me out.” We all chuckled nervously as we looked at each other.

We heard a creak and turned to look as the door was closing all by itself. It moved slowly across its arc. We watched quietly for a few seconds. The door shut and with a soft click, latched itself. We all looked at each other inquisitively and started laughing.

Karen informed us, “The AR says a time limit has been reached. We can open the door again in 5 minutes.”

Susan shook her head, “Ah …I’ll pass.”

“What about you guys, ready to go?”

Karen nodded, “Yeah, this thing gives me the willies. It’s like a hole in reality and it almost felt like it was watching us,” her body moved with a slight shiver.

We had seen the whole show and agreed to start walking back to the transportation zone for pickup. As we meandered through the large pedestrian areas, we talked about the various pieces and how it was heartening to see human works starting to make a comeback.

“I thought the zen rock garden was hilarious,” said David.

It was a large piece about the size of a pool table with fine sand raked in contours around the rocks like a zen garden. But instead of actual rocks there were miniature models of rock n’ roll instruments like drums, guitars, keyboards ….

“My AR asked to pick a song. So I chose an old rock-and-roll. Then these holographic projections of the band’s actual musicians faded onto the toy instruments and started playing. The best part was when the lead singer faded onto the empty rock in the middle of the garden, you know the one that was shaped like a star. Get it, the rock star. That had me cracking up. Hah!” David laughed with a big grin while he was shaking his head at the puns.

Susan added, “That one was fun, you were jamming out to one of your oldies. And I had another song playing for me. It was that old pop song, you guys remember the one.” Susan started singing and swaying to the melody …

“Yes, you still got it Sis!”, said Karen, interrupting Susan’s rendition before she really got going.

“But, oh my gosh, you guys remember the room with all the monitors that were setup at weird angles? I thought that one was the best—the images it pulled from the gallery’s cameras and the way it played them back was like it told a story just with people’s facial expressions.”

It was a system that used facial recognition to analyze the real time video feed of all the people in the gallery. It continually updated its database with the image of a person right at the moment of their most pronounced facial expression, whatever it was; happy, sad, surprised, confused, curious. Then an AI took the stream of constantly updated images and placed them on all the different monitors. It positioned them in a such a way that you could imagine all sorts of drama.

“Oh god, I don’t know how it got that image of me laughing with my mouth wide open,” said Susan.

I just couldn’t resist and in a dead pan tone said, “Not hard to do, your mouth is always open.”

Everyone paused for a moment and then, thankfully, we all laughed.

We walked on to the transportation zone’s departure area and waited in the outgoing queue. I activated my AR and quickly selected the number of passengers and our home address as the destination.

As we said our goodbyes, David added, “See you guys tomorrow night right?”

“I sent you the address. It’s at my old boss Danny’s place. It’s pretty swanky, but we’re all blue collar so it’s going to be casual. Lots of good food and drinks. Oh, they even have a big Jacuzzi and heated pool so bring your swim gear.”

Karen responded, ”Don’t worry, weĺl be there! I’m doing a long shift at the hospital tomorrow and will be ready to unwind.“

Susan got excited, “There’ll be some local musicians there too. We can dance like the old days Sis!” She grabbed Karen’s hands, started singing, and they started moving together to Susan’s voice.

Our transport arrived just in time. That evening, Karen and I relaxed over dinner that I made from the local farmer co-op’s aquaponic fish and fresh produce. I had placed the order for the groceries on the ride home. They were delivered with plenty of time to cook.

“What’s up Paul?”, asked Karen as she took a bite of salad and looked at me inquisitively.

“Hmm, oh,” I must’ve been spacing off again, “I was just thinking of the old days. One project after another. Five pans in the fire at any given moment.” I sighed a little, “Guess I’m missing having ownership and the feeling of accomplishment that work used to bring.”

“Baby, you contribute a lot to our life together. And look how you helped those artists with that wonderful show!”

She always seemed to know how to re-frame my pessimistic ruminations into something more positive. “You’re right honey, thanks. A lot of times I do get excited about helping out but it feels like I’m just getting the scraps. The AIs handle all the major decisions. It’s like I lost my mojo or something. Thank goodness I have the co-op, my projects, and you to keep me busy.” as I smiled and peered into her dark eyes.

“Yeah, you are pretty lucky.” said Karen with a big grin.

“I am at that. Hey, look, we’re all done with dinner. How about you grab the wine. We can relax a little before bed.”

“Mmm, that sounds good,” as Karen ran her hands behind her neck and shook her hair out. I watched her get up and slink away slowly. She went around the corner and glanced back with a sultry look.

I was sitting in my normal meditation posture. On the floor, on top of a small cushion, legs slightly crossed, hands in my lap with the palms facing up. Left hand resting on top of the right, eyes closed and facing straight ahead. I lowered my head down and opened my eyes. It was really dark, like I was in a cave or something, but I didn’t feel cold. I blinked a couple of times and sat for a minute. Slowly I could start to see the faint outline of my hands poking out of my long sleeved shirt. I brought them a little closer and flipped them over and back again. They looked black and white, like all the color was drained out of them.

My hands floated back down to my lap. I could make out the faint outline of my legs. My feet were bare and were looking very pale and colorless like my hands. From my feet I tried to make out the ground. There was just an unsettling flat black that I was sitting on. I moved my hand down to touch the ground and it was slightly warm to the touch and felt very smooth. With my hand on the ground I could see that my long sleeved shirt was a dark color, black I thought, but what was below was even darker.

I thought of the Mondoblack© display. A shade of deep, complete and utter black. And here it was. I was sitting right on top of a whole world of it. My gaze slowly moved upward and the black ground went on and on. I looked up enough to see the distant horizon and breathed a sigh of relief as the stars were out. They were the source of the faint light. The stars that were at the border of the black ground looked like they had been laser cut in half.

As my head tilted higher, I saw the beautiful Milky Way stretched out overhead. I hadn’t seen stars so bright and so clear for a long time. It was many years ago on a cold night at a dark sky preserve up in central Idaho that I had seen such a wonder. Here they seemed clearer, sharper somehow and at least I wasn’t cold. The arc of the galaxy and the millions of stars filled the whole sky in an amazing display. I stared for a while and then turned my gaze. All around me was just more Mondoblack©. The doorway at the art show looked like it was cut out of reality. Here it was like the whole universe was cut out, and I was sitting on the dark side of what was left.

My hands seemed to float as if there was nothing behind them. I had no depth perception looking down at that black. I turned my gaze back to night sky and suddenly took in a quick gasp of air. The stars were starting to disappear! The flat horizon, the blackness had started to grow higher. It had been straight ahead, but now I had to raise my head to see where the stars started. It was eating up the sky.

Wait, no, it wasn’t moving up. My breath quickened as I came to the realization—I was sinking down into the black! I struggled to get up out of my seat but my legs wouldn’t move. It was like everything below my waist was encased in concrete. I tried to push up with my hands but the ground had gone away. I was slowly floating down into the black.

The faint light was waning as more and more sky was being consumed by the black. It was climbing faster as, like on the inside of a sphere, there was less area it had to consume. My breath was racing and I felt panic as claustrophobia was kicking in.

I had to talk myself down, so I took a deep breath and thought, “You can’t get out right now, can you? You’re just scared and that’s understandable. Maybe the only way out is through? Take deep slow breaths. It’ll help you calm down and think—that’s it, slow it down.”

After a minute, I felt calmer. As the black was reaching the stars directly above me I looked around for anything else that I grab onto, but it was turning to total darkness and to that unsettling empty flat black. The circle of stars above me was growing smaller and smaller. I couldn’t take my eyes off of the shrinking light. Slowly it closed in and the stars disappeared without a sound. The last of the faint light was gone. I was in total darkness.

I felt myself getting more anxious and afraid. The black was all around me and it felt so close. All I could hear was the beating of my heart and my hurried breath. My claustrophobia was worsening. I struggled to get up again but my legs were locked and I couldn’t move anything below my waist.

Above me, where the stars had been, was only blackness. I didn’t expect to see anything different, but now, very far in the distance in front of me, there was an extremely small pinpoint of light. It had a slightly golden hue and twinkled like a star but seemed different somehow. Each shard of light that emanated out took the shape of a clean elongated piercing triangle. Being in the Mondoblack© made it easier to differentiate the unusual light at the center of this dark universe I found myself in.

It felt like I was moving, but was still planted in my seat. There was a feeling of subtle queasiness. Similar to what I felt looking at the Mondoblack© doorway at the art show. I started to hear a sound, like water in a stream, but it was very faint and far away. My ears strained to hear but I couldn’t make it any clearer.

I wish that light would get brighter! It was the one thing in this dark place that held the possibility of a way out. I wanted to run but still couldn’t move out of my goddamn seat. The light held there. It felt like I was moving—no …not toward it, I was circling it somehow. I don’t know how I knew, but I was in a huge orbit, moving slowly around the center of this black place, so slowly.

My breathing quickened and I strained my muscles but nothing was working. The faint light and quiet sound of water were unchanged. I was feeling dizzy again like in the gallery, like after spinning in a circle. The light was going in and out of focus. I tried to keep my eyes on it, but everything was swimming.
